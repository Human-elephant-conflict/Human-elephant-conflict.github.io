Solutions for Human-Elephant Conflict

    Addressing the human-elephant conflict in Yunnan, China, requires a multifaceted approach that combines innovative technologies, community engagement, and international cooperation. The escalating interactions between humans and Asian elephants, driven by habitat loss and expanding human development, have necessitated the implementation of effective solutions to protect both the local communities and the elephant populations. This section explores comprehensive strategies designed to mitigate these conflicts, including the deployment of wire fence alarm systems, the establishment of advanced early warning systems, the expansion of international cooperation for joint protection efforts, and the provision of fair compensation for damages. By integrating these measures, we aim to foster a harmonious coexistence between humans and elephants, ensuring the sustainability of both human livelihoods and elephant conservation.

Wire Fence Alarm System

The Wildlife Conservation Society (WCS) Thailand project and national park managers found that an effective method is the wire fence alarm system, which prevents elephants from passing with an effectiveness of 73%.

Implementation: In areas where elephants pose a threat, wooden posts are installed approximately every 20 meters, with two wires strung at heights of 90 cm and 130 cm to avoid obstructing smaller animals. At each corner of the fence, an alarm and lighting system are installed every 400 meters, along with a watchtower every 800 meters for guards to scare off elephants. When elephants attempt to enter farmland from the forest, alarms are triggered, and lights illuminate to drive them away.

Early Warning System

In 2018, to better monitor elephants leaving protected areas and approaching farmland and villages, a monitoring and early warning center for Asian elephants was established in Menghai County, Xishuangbanna. This system provides safety for people's travel and production activities.

Currently, there are over 300 infrared cameras installed throughout the Xishuangbanna region, with more than 200 located around protected areas. When elephants are detected, their location and other information are automatically published on the Xishuangbanna Elephant Early Warning System App. A broadcast warning is then issued to villages in the vicinity, alerting them to the potential presence of elephants.

Expanding International Cooperation and Joint Protection

Strengthening international cooperation to establish a joint protection framework for Asian elephants across 13 countries in South and Southeast Asia. Joining the "Convention on Migratory Species" will help remove barriers to elephant migration and promote collaborative protection among countries in the elephants' range.

Compensation for Damages

Even in cases of damage, Pacific Insurance will assess and provide compensation based on individual losses. Currently, compensation for fatalities caused by wild elephants in Xishuangbanna is set at 400,000 yuan per person. Crop damages will be compensated according to the type of crop.

Challenges in Solutions

Current solutions for mitigating human-elephant conflict face significant challenges. High costs prevent the installation of protective fences around every village, let alone farmlands, to stop elephant damage. Infrared cameras are often destroyed or avoided by elephants, and without on-the-ground observers, early warning systems in rural areas sometimes fail to alert villagers in time. Additionally, the economic compensation for farmers' losses covers less than one-third of market value, and damages to homes remain uncompensated, with delays in assessments and payments adding to the frustration.


Elephant behavior varies depending on the region's environment and culture, complicating the relationship between humans and elephants. In China, where there are "more people and fewer elephants," their fragmented habitats are surrounded by human settlements, leading to frequent encounters. In Malaysia, with "fewer people and more elephants," plantations dominate, causing elephants to damage crops but with fewer human interactions. In Sri Lanka, where both populations are high, human-elephant conflict is frequent, and despite the local love for elephants, rising tensions lead to lethal responses. Addressing the conflict requires a nuanced understanding of not only the elephants but also the people and environments they live in.
